\section{General Information}Stokes flow, also named creeping flow or creeping motion, is a type of fluid flow where advective inertial forces are negligibly small compared with viscous forces. This regime is mathematically characterized by a low Reynolds number, i.e., 
\[
\mathrm{Re} = \frac{\rho U L}{\mu} \ll 1 \quad \nu=\frac{\mu}{\rho},
\]
where $\rho$ is the fluid density, $U$ is a characteristic velocity scale, $L$ is a characteristic length scale, $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity, and $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity. This situation is highly typical in applications where the fluid velocities are remarkably slow, the fluid viscosities are exceptionally large, or the geometric length-scales of the flow domain are micro- or nano-sized. 

Creeping flow was historically first analyzed by Sir George Gabriel Stokes to model the settling of spheres and understand hydrodynamic lubrication. In nature, this type of flow governs the swimming mechanics of microorganisms, the movement of lava, and the slow mantle convection currents deep within the Earth. In engineering and biotechnology, it occurs prominently in painting processes, micro-electromechanical systems (MEMS), microfluidic lab-on-a-chip devices, and the transport of viscous polymers during extrusion.

\subsection{Mathematical Characterization and Challenges}
From a mathematical perspective, the Stokes equations are obtained by simplifying the non-linear incompressible Navier-Stokes equations: 

\[
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\dfrac{1}{\rho} \nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{f}\\[0.8em]
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 
\end{cases}
\]

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{u}$ is the flow velocity vector field.
    \item $t$ represents time.
    \item $p$ is the scalar static pressure field.
    \item $\rho$ is the constant fluid density.
    \item $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity.
    \item $\mathbf{f}$ represents external body force accelerations (such as gravity).
\end{itemize}

By dropping the convective inertial acceleration term $(\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}$, the momentum equation can be linearized, transforming into a system where the velocity field $\mathbf{u}$ and the pressure field $p$ are coupled strictly through viscous dissipation and incompressibility constraints.

The absence of a time-derivative or non-linear term simplifies some analytical aspects but introduces profound structural numerical challenges for the Finite Element Method:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Saddle-Point Structure:} The incompressibility constraint $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$ acts as a structural constraint on the velocity space, giving the weak formulation a classic \textit{saddle-point} (or constrained optimization) algebraic structure.
    \item \textbf{The Inf-Sup (LBB) Condition:} Unlike standard elliptic systems (such as the Poisson equation), one cannot arbitrarily pair discrete finite element approximation spaces for velocity ($X_h$) and pressure ($Q_h$). Unstable combinations lead to spurious numerical pressure oscillations (``checkerboard'' pressure fields). To guarantee stability and convergence, the discrete spaces must satisfy the strict \textit{Ladyzhenskaya-Babu\v{s}ka-Brezzi (LBB)} or \textit{inf-sup} condition, requiring deliberate pairings such as the Taylor-Hood element ($P_k / P_{k-1}$) or stabilized formulations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stable Macro-Element Discretizations: The \texorpdfstring{$P_1\text{-iso-}P_1$}{P1-iso-P1} Element}
To circumvent the structural instabilities imposed by the inf-sup condition without resorting to complex higher-order polynomial expansions or non-conforming spaces, mixed finite element frameworks can leverage hierarchical macro-element structures. A prominent and computationally efficient choice within this class is the $P_1\text{-iso-}P_1$ element pair, historically introduced by Bercovier and Pironneau.

Instead of varying the polynomial degree between variables on a single mesh, the $P_1\text{-iso-}P_1$ method varies the underlying triangulation density while keeping both velocity and pressure spaces continuously piecewise linear ($P_1$). The discretization relies on a coarse macro-mesh $\mathcal{T}_H$ and a fine mesh $\mathcal{T}_h$. The fine mesh is constructed via a uniform refinement protocol where each parent macro-triangle $K \in \mathcal{T}_H$ is subdivided into four congruent child triangles by connecting the midpoints of its three topological edges, effectively halving the characteristic mesh size such that $h = H/2$.

The discrete spaces for the fluid variables are then defined as:
\begin{align*}
X_h &= \{ \mathbf{v}_h \in C^0(\overline{\Omega})^2 : \mathbf{v}_h|_K \in P_1(K)^2, \forall K \in \mathcal{T}_h \} \\
Q_h &= \{ q_h \in C^0(\overline{\Omega}) : q_h|_M \in P_1(M), \forall M \in \mathcal{T}_H \}
\end{align*}
By defining the velocity field $\mathbf{u}_h$ on the refined grid $\mathcal{T}_h$ and the pressure field $p_h$ on the coarse macro-grid $\mathcal{T}_H$, the velocity space is enriched with a substantial number of internal degrees of freedom. This spatial scale separation provides a stable velocity-to-pressure ratio that satisfies the discrete Ladyzhenskaya-Babu\v{s}ka-Brezzi (LBB) condition:

\[
\exists \beta>0: \quad
\inf_{q \in Q_{\setminus\{0\}}} \space \sup_{v \in V_{\setminus\{0\}}} \left(\frac{\int_{\Omega}q(\nabla \cdot v)d\Omega}{\|v\|_{V}\cdot\|q\|_{Q}}\right) \ge \beta 
\]

where the stability constant $\beta > 0$ remains strictly bounded away from zero independently of the mesh refinement level. This macro-element architecture yields a highly symmetric structural layout, allowing the numerical engine to enjoy the low computational overhead of linear shape functions while retaining robust convergence properties.

\section{Strong Formulation}

The steady-state behavior of an incompressible, highly viscous fluid where inertial forces are negligible relative to viscous friction is governed by the classical Stokes equations. This system of partial differential equations forms a fundamental linear model in fluid mechanics. Given a bounded domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ enclosed by a boundary $\partial\Omega = \Gamma_D \cup \Gamma_N$ (where $\Gamma_D \cap \Gamma_N = \emptyset$), the strong formulation of the problem seeks the velocity field $\mathbf{u}(x,y)$ and the scalar pressure field $p(x,y)$ satisfying:

\[
\begin{cases}
-\nu \Delta u + \nabla p = 0
& \text{in } \Omega, \\[0.5em]

\nabla \cdot u = 0
& \text{in } \Omega, \\[0.5em]

u = g
& \text{on } \Gamma_D, \\[0.5em]

-p\,n + \nu (\nabla u)n = h
& \text{on } \Gamma_N.
\end{cases}
\]

The mathematical and physical roles of these individual components are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Momentum Balance ($-\nu \Delta \mathbf{u} + \nabla p = \mathbf{0}$):} This represents the conservation of momentum. It dictates a strict equilibrium between the internal viscous shear stresses—scaled by the kinematic viscosity $\nu$—and the driving force generated by the pressure gradient $\nabla p$.
    \item \textbf{Incompressibility Constraint ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$):} This serves as the conservation of mass equation for an incompressible fluid. It requires the velocity field to be divergence-free, ensuring that fluid volume is perfectly preserved at every point within the domain.
    \item \textbf{Dirichlet Boundary Condition ($\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{g}$):} Enforced on the boundary segment $\Gamma_D$, this prescribes the exact fluid velocity. It is commonly used to dictate non-zero flow profiles at fluid inlets or to impose the physical ``no-slip'' condition ($\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}$) along stationary solid walls.
    \item \textbf{Neumann Boundary Condition ($-p\mathbf{n} + \nu (\nabla \mathbf{u})\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{h}$):} Enforced on the boundary segment $\Gamma_N$ with outward unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$, this specifies the weak normal stress or traction force vector $\mathbf{h}$ acting on the fluid, typically representing free-pressure outlets or open boundaries.
\end{itemize}

\section{Weak Formulation}

To solve the Stokes system using the Finite Element Method, we transform the original strong partial differential equations into their weak (or variational) integral form. This is achieved by multiplying the momentum and continuity equations by smooth test functions, $\mathbf{v}$ and $q$, and integrating the terms over the entire physical domain $\Omega$. Applying integration by parts (Green's identity) to the second-order viscous diffusion term reduces the continuity requirements on our velocity fields from second-order derivatives down to first-order derivatives. 

During this mathematical transition, the natural boundary conditions—such as the traction force $h$ on the Neumann boundary $\Gamma_N$—automatically emerge within the boundary integrals. To accommodate non-zero (inhomogeneous) Dirichlet velocity profiles on the walls while strictly maintaining a homogeneous test function space $X$ where $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ on $\Gamma_D$, we apply a boundary lifting technique. We decompose the true velocity field into $\mathbf{u}_{total} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{r}_g$, where $\mathbf{r}_g$ is a known extension function matching the boundary data, and $\mathbf{u} \in X$ is the remaining homogeneous unknown field. This systematic process yields the coupled weak formulation system:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stokes_weak}
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle
\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla u : \nabla v \, d\Omega
-
\int_{\Omega} p \, (\nabla \cdot v)\, d\Omega
=
\int_{\Gamma_N} h \cdot v \, d\Gamma
-
\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla r_g : \nabla v \, d\Omega,
& \forall v \in X,
\\[1.2em]

\displaystyle
-\int_{\Omega} q \, (\nabla \cdot u)\, d\Omega
=
\int_{\Omega} q \, (\nabla \cdot r_g)\, d\Omega,
& \forall q \in Q.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\section{Finite Element Discretization and Matrix Assembly}

In this section, we transition from the continuous \textbf{weak formulation} to a \textbf{discrete algebraic representation} suitable for computational execution. By projecting the infinite-dimensional solution space onto a finite-dimensional subspace spanned by piecewise linear basis functions, we transform the governing integral operators into global sparse matrices. The following subsections detail the systematic assembly of these components.

\subsection{Reference Triangle}

To compute integrals efficiently, we map every arbitrary triangle in our mesh onto a standard master element called the reference triangle $\hat{T}$ in local coordinates $(\xi_1, \xi_2)$. Its vertices are fixed at $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_1 = (0,0)$, $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_2 = (1,0)$, and $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_3 = (0,1)$. On this master domain, we define the standard linear Lagrange shape functions $\hat{\phi}_i(\xi_1, \xi_2)$:


\begin{tcolorbox}[
    colframe=RoyalBlue, 
    colback=RoyalBlue!7!white, 
    sharp corners
]
\begin{center}
$
\begin{aligned}
\displaystyle
    \hat{\phi}_1(\xi_1, \xi_2) &= 1 - \xi_1 - \xi_2 &\hspace{113pt}
    & 
    \hat{\phi}_1(0, 0) = 1, \quad 
    \hat{\phi}_1(1, 0) = 0, \quad
    \hat{\phi}_1(0, 1) = 0
    \\[5pt]
    \hat{\phi}_2(\xi_1, \xi_2) &= \xi_1 &\hspace{113pt}
    &
    \hat{\phi}_2(0, 0) = 0, \quad 
    \hat{\phi}_2(1, 0) = 1, \quad
    \hat{\phi}_2(0, 1) = 0
    \\[5pt]
    \hat{\phi}_3(\xi_1, \xi_2) &= \xi_2 &\hspace{113pt}
    &
    \hat{\phi}_3(0, 0) = 0, \quad 
    \hat{\phi}_3(1, 0) = 0, \quad
    \hat{\phi}_3(0, 1) = 1
    \\[5pt]
\end{aligned}
$
\end{center}
\end{tcolorbox}

It is also necessary to take a look at the original shape functions' derivatives:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:grad_for_Bx_By}
\begin{aligned}
    \nabla \phi_i(x,y) &=
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \phi _i}{\partial x}\\[5pt]
        \frac{\partial \phi _i}{\partial y}
    \end{bmatrix}
    = J_T^{-T} \cdot \nabla \hat{\phi}_i(\xi_1, \xi_2)
    = \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
       x_2 - x_1 & x_3 - x_1 \\
       y_2 - y_1 & y_3 - y_1
    \end{array}
    \right]
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial x}\\[5pt]
        \frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial y}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \\[10pt]
    &= |J_T|^{-1}
        \begin{bmatrix}            
        \quad (y_3 - y_1)\frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial \xi _1} - (y_2 - y_1)\frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial \xi _2}\\[5pt]
        \space-(x_3 - x_1)\frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial \xi _1} + (x_2 - x_1)\frac{\partial \hat{\phi} _i}{\partial \xi _2}
        \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Stiffness Matrix \texorpdfstring{$\mathbf{A}$}{A}}

To determine the discrete operator for the viscous term, we begin with the bilinear form involving the velocity test and trial functions:

\begin{tcolorbox}[
    colframe=ForestGreen, 
    colback=ForestGreen!7!white, 
    sharp corners
]
\begin{center}
   $\displaystyle \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla u : \nabla v \, d\Omega$ 
\end{center}
\end{tcolorbox}

\[
\begin{aligned}    
\displaystyle
\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \mathbf{u}(x,y) : \nabla \mathbf{v}(x,y)  \, d\Omega &= 
\left|\begin{array}{c}
   \mathbf{u}(x,y) =\begin{bmatrix}
       u_x(x,y) \\
       u_y(x,y) 
   \end{bmatrix}
   \\[17pt]
   \mathbf{v}(x,y) =\begin{bmatrix}
       v_x(x,y) \\
       v_y(x,y) 
   \end{bmatrix}
\end{array}\right|
=\int_{\Omega} \nu \left(\nabla u_x \cdot \nabla v_x + \nabla u_y \cdot \nabla v_y \right) \, d\Omega= \\[10pt]
&= \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla u_x \cdot \nabla v_x \, d\Omega +\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla u_y \cdot \nabla v_y \, d\Omega 
= \left|\begin{array}{c}
u_x(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i\phi_i(x,y) \\[17pt]
u_y(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i\phi_i(x,y) 
\end{array} \right|= \\[10pt]
&=\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \left(\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i\phi_i \right) \cdot \nabla v_x \, d\Omega +\int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \left(\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i\phi_i \right) \cdot \nabla v_y \, d\Omega= \\[10pt]
&= \left[\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i \cdot \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \phi_i \cdot \nabla v_x \, d\Omega\right] + \left[\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i \cdot \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \phi_i \cdot \nabla v_y \, d\Omega\right] = \\[10pt]
&=\left|\substack{\displaystyle\text{We now set $v_x(x,y) = \phi_j(x,y)$} \\ \displaystyle\text{and $v_y(x,y) = \phi_j(x,y)$}}\right|=\\[10pt]
&=\left[\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i \cdot \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \phi_i \cdot \nabla \phi_j \, d\Omega\right] + \left[\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i \cdot \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \phi_i \cdot \nabla \phi_j \, d\Omega\right]=\\[10pt]
&=\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}A_{ji} u^x_i  + \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}A_{ji} u^y_i = \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{u}_x + \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{u}_y = \left|\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}^T\right| = \\[10pt]
&= \mathbf{A} \mathbf{u}_x + \mathbf{A} \mathbf{u}_y
=\left[ \begin{array}{cc}
    \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{A}
\end{array}\right] 
\begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{u}_x\\
    \mathbf{u}_y
\end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
\]


\subsection{Pressure Matrices \texorpdfstring{$\mathbf{B}_x$ and $\mathbf{B}_y$}{Bx and By}}

To enforce the incompressibility constraint and couple the pressure field to the momentum equations, we discretize the divergence of the velocity field field mixed with the pressure test space. This interaction is governed by the bilinear form:

\begin{tcolorbox}[
    colframe=ForestGreen, 
    colback=ForestGreen!7!white, 
    sharp corners
]
\begin{center}
   $\displaystyle -\int_{\Omega} p \, (\nabla \cdot v)\, d\Omega$ 
\end{center}
\end{tcolorbox}

By splitting the velocity test function into its component channels $\mathbf{v} = [v_x, v_y]^T$ and expanding the fields using the mixed finite element bases ($\phi_i$ for velocity and $\psi_j$ for pressure), this integral yields two distinct rectangular coupling matrices. The matrix $\mathbf{B}_x$ maps the $x$-velocity components to the pressure nodes, while $\mathbf{B}_y$ handles the $y$-velocity components, forming the weak gradient and divergence operations in our discrete system.

\[
\begin{aligned}
\displaystyle
-\int_{\Omega} p \, (\nabla \cdot v)\, d\Omega 
&= \left| 
\begin{array}{c}
     p(x,y) \approx \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \psi_j \\[15pt]
     \mathbf{v}(x,y) =\begin{bmatrix}
       v_x(x,y) \\
       v_y(x,y) 
   \end{bmatrix}
\end{array}\right|
= - \int_\Omega \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \psi_j
\begin{bmatrix}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\\[1pt]
    \frac{\partial}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix}
\cdot 
\begin{bmatrix}
    v_x\\[7pt]
    v_y
\end{bmatrix} \, d\Omega = \\[10pt]
&= \left[\sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \cdot \left(-\int_\Omega \psi_j \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} \, d_\Omega\right)\right] + \left[ \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \cdot \left(-\int_\Omega \psi_j \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} \, d_\Omega\right)\right]
= \left|\begin{array}{c}
    v_x(x,y) = \phi_i(x,y)\\
    v_y(x,y) = \phi_i(x,y)
\end{array}\right| =\\[10pt]
&= \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \cdot \left(-\int_\Omega \psi_j \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial x} \, d_\Omega\right) + \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \cdot \left(-\int_\Omega \psi_j \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial y} \, d_\Omega\right)
= \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \left(B_x\right)_{ij} + \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j \left(B_y\right)_{ij}=\\[10pt]
&= \mathbf{B}_x^T\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{B}_y^T\mathbf{p}
=\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    \mathbf{B}_x^T & \mathbf{B}_y^T
\end{array}
\right] 
\begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{p}\\
    \mathbf{p}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
\]


\subsection{Saddle-Point Block Matrix \texorpdfstring{$\mathbf{K}$}{K}}

By organizing the viscous diffusion blocks and the divergence constraint blocks into a single monolithic operator, we construct the global saddle-point system. This specific structural arrangement takes the classic Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) form:

% Pressure acts like a lagrange multiplier!!!

\[
\left[
\begin{array}{ccc}
\mathbf{A} & 0 & \mathbf{B}_x^T \\
0 & \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B}_y^T \\
\mathbf{B}_x & \mathbf{B}_y & 0
\end{array}
\right]
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{u}_x \\
\mathbf{u}_y \\
\mathbf{p}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{F}_x \\
\mathbf{F}_y \\
\mathbf{0}
\end{bmatrix}
\]

The block layout reflects the underlying \textbf{mixed formulation}~\eqref{eq:stokes_weak}, where the diagonal velocity stiffness blocks $\mathbf{A}$ remain symmetric positive definite, while the zero block on the lower diagonal highlights the lack of an explicit pressure time-dependence or compressibility stabilizer. The dimensional layout of these individual blocks scales according to the total degrees of freedom allocated to each field variable:

\[
\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_u \times N_u} \quad
\mathbf{B}_x \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times N_u} \quad
\mathbf{B}_x^T \in \mathbb{R}^{N_u \times N_p}
\]

\subsection{Load Vector \texorpdfstring{$\mathbf{F}$}{F} Assembly and Inhomogeneous Boundary Lifting}

\[
\mathbf{F} = \int_{\Gamma_N} h \cdot v \, d\Gamma - \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla r_g : \nabla v \, d\Omega = \mathbf{F}_{forces} - \mathbf{A}\vec{r_g} = 
\left[
    \begin{array}{c}
    F_x\\
    F_y
    \end{array}
\right] - \mathbf{A}\vec{r_g}
\]

\newpage
\section{Ladyzhenskaya–Babuška–Brezzi Condition}
In numerical partial differential equations, the \textit{Ladyzhenskaya–Babuška–Brezzi }(LBB) condition is a sufficient condition for a saddle point problem to have a unique solution that depends continuously on the input data.

At the continuous level, the velocity space $V$ and pressure space $Q$ must satisfy:

\[
\exists \beta>0: \quad
\inf_{q \in Q_{\setminus\{0\}}} \space \sup_{v \in V_{\setminus\{0\}}} \left(\frac{\int_{\Omega}q(\nabla \cdot v)d\Omega}{\|v\|_{V}\cdot\|q\|_{Q}}\right) \ge \beta 
\]

\subsection{Discrete Mapping and Coordinate Representation}
For this problem it is useful to reformulate the condition in a discrete way. We can start by mapping the continuous functions to their nodal coordinate representations:


\[
v \rightarrow \mathbf{u}=
\begin{bmatrix}
    u_1^x\\
    \vdots\\
    u_{N_v}^x\\
    u_1^y\\
    \vdots\\
    u_{N_v}^y
\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{2N_v},
\quad 
q \rightarrow \mathbf{p}=
\begin{bmatrix}
    p_1\\
    \vdots\\
    p_{N_p}
\end{bmatrix}\in \mathbb{R}^{N_p}\\[10pt]
\]
The corresponding discrete differential bilinear forms translate directly into the global block matrices assembled across the mesh:

\small
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
    \displaystyle
    \mathbf{A}_{ij} = \int_{\Omega} \nu \nabla \phi_i(x,y) \cdot \nabla \phi_j(x,y) \, d\Omega \quad & 
    \displaystyle \left(\mathbf{B}_x\right)_{ij} = -\int_{\Omega} \psi_j(x,y)\frac{\partial \phi_i(x,y)}{\partial x} \, d\Omega \quad & 
    \displaystyle \left(\mathbf{B}_y\right)_{ij} = -\int_{\Omega} \psi_j(x,y) \frac{\partial \phi_i(x,y)}{\partial y} \, d\Omega \\[15pt]
    \displaystyle \mathbf{B}=\left[\mathbf{B}_x \quad\mathbf{B}_y\right] \quad & \displaystyle \mathbf{M}_v=\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}
\end{array}
\right] \quad & \displaystyle \left(\mathbf{M}_p\right)_{ij} = \int_{\Omega} \psi_j(x,y)\psi_i(x,y)\, d\Omega
\end{array}
\]
\normalsize

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^{2N_v}$ is the velocity vector on a fine grid;
    \item $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p}$ is the pressure vector on a coarse grid;
    \item $\mathbf{B}_x \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times N_v}$ is the discrete horizontal divergence operator;
    \item $\mathbf{B}_y \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times N_v}$ is the discrete vertical divergence operator;
    \item $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times 2N_v}$ is a discrete divergence operator, responsible for coupling pressure and velocity;
    \item $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_v \times N_v}$ is a discrete Laplacian operator that handles the viscosity of the fluid;
    \item $\mathbf{M}_v \in \mathbb{R}^{2N_v \times 2N_v}$ is the velocity energy weight matrix, used to calculate the physical size and kinetic energy of the velocity field.
    \item $\mathbf{M}_p \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p \times N_p}$ is the pressure mass matrix that computes the spatial overlap of pressure basis functions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discretization Proofs for Operators and Field Norms}
Using the coordinate definitions above, we can systematically transform the three core components of the continuous inf-sup condition into algebraic matrix operations:

\begingroup
\small
\begin{enumerate}
    \raggedright
    % Write out in Bx and By and then combine and v_x, v_y have to be used
    \item $\begin{aligned}[t]
        \displaystyle\int_{\Omega}q(\nabla \cdot v) \, d\Omega &= \left|
        \begin{array}{ccc}
            q(x,y)\approx \displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j\psi_j(x,y) & v_x(x,y)\approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i\phi_i(x,y) & \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\phi_i(x,y)\\
            v(x,y)=
            \begin{bmatrix}
               v_x(x,y) \\
               v_y(x,y)  
            \end{bmatrix} & v_y(x,y)\approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i\phi_i(x,y) & \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y}\approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\phi_i(x,y)
        \end{array}\right| = \\[10pt]
        &= \int_{\Omega}q(x,y) \left(
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\\
            \frac{\partial}{\partial y}
        \end{bmatrix}^T
        \begin{bmatrix}
            v_x(x,y) \\
            v_y(x,y)
        \end{bmatrix}
        \right) \, d\Omega 
        = \int_{\Omega} q(x,y)\left( \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} \right) \, d\Omega = \\[10pt]
        &= \int_{\Omega} q(x,y)\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} \, d\Omega
        + \int_{\Omega} q(x,y)\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} \, d\Omega = \\[10pt]
        &= \int_{\Omega} \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j\psi_j(x,y) \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^x_i\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\phi_i(x,y) \, d\Omega + \int_{\Omega} \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j\psi_j(x,y) \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u^y_i\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\phi_i(x,y) \, d\Omega = \\[10pt]
        &= \left[\sum_{j=1}^{N_p}\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}p_j \cdot \int_{\Omega}\psi_j(x,y) \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\phi_i(x,y) \, d\Omega \cdot u^x_i \right] + \left[ \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}p_j \cdot \int_{\Omega}\psi_j(x,y) \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\phi_i(x,y) \, d\Omega \cdot u^y_i \right] \\[10pt]
        &= \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}\sum_{i=1}^{N_v} p_j \left(B_x\right)_{ji} u^x_i + \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}\sum_{i=1}^{N_v} p_j\left(B_y\right)_{ji} u^y_i = \mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B}_x \mathbf{u}_x + \mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B}_y \mathbf{u}_y = \mathbf{p}^T \left[\mathbf{B}_x \quad \mathbf{B}_y\right]
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \mathbf{u}_x \\
            \mathbf{u}_y
        \end{bmatrix} = \\[10pt]
        &=\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B} \mathbf{u}
        \end{aligned}$

    \item $\begin{aligned}[t]
    \|v\|_V &= \left( \displaystyle\int_{\Omega} \left( \|\nabla v_x\|^2 + \|\nabla v_y\|^2 \right) \, d\Omega \right)^{1/2}
    = \left( \int_{\Omega} \left( \nabla v_x \cdot \nabla v_x + \nabla v_y \cdot \nabla v_y \right) \, d\Omega \right)^{1/2} =\\
    &= 
    \left|
    \begin{array}{cc}
         v_x(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u_i^x\phi_i(x,y) & \nabla v_x(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u_i^x\nabla\phi_i(x,y)\\[10pt]
         \quad v_y(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{N_v}u_j^y\phi_j(x,y) & \nabla v_y(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{N_v}u_j^y\nabla\phi_j(x,y)
    \end{array}
    \right| =\\
    &= \left( \displaystyle\int_{\Omega} \left(\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u_i^x\nabla\phi_i(x,y) \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{N_v}u_j^x\nabla\phi_j(x,y) + \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}u_i^y\nabla\phi_i(x,y) \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{N_v}u_j^y\nabla\phi_j(x,y) \right) \, d\Omega \right)^{1/2} =\\
    &= \left(\left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}\sum_{j=1}^{N_v} u_i^x \cdot \displaystyle\int_{\Omega} \nabla\phi_i(x,y) \cdot \nabla\phi_j(x,y)\, d\Omega \cdot u_j^x\right] + \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}\sum_{j=1}^{N_v}\left[u_i^y\cdot \displaystyle\int_{\Omega} \nabla\phi_i(x,y) \cdot \nabla\phi_j(x,y)\, d\Omega \cdot u_j^y\right]\right)^{1/2} = \\[10pt]
    &= \displaystyle\left(\left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N_v}\sum_{j=1}^{N_v} u_i^x A_{ij}  u_j^x\right] + \left[\sum_{i=1}^{N_v}\sum_{j=1}^{N_v}u_i^y A_{ij} u_j^y\right]\right)^{1/2} = \\[10pt]
    &= \left(\mathbf{u_x}^T \mathbf{A} \mathbf{u_x} + \mathbf{u_y}^T \mathbf{A} \mathbf{u_y}\right)^{1/2} = \left(
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \mathbf{u_x}\\
        \mathbf{u_y}
    \end{bmatrix}^T
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{0} \\
        \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}
    \end{array}
    \right]
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \mathbf{u_x}\\
        \mathbf{u_y}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \right)^{1/2}
    = \left(\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{M}_v \mathbf{u}\right)^{1/2}
    \end{aligned}$
    
    \item $\begin{aligned}[t]
        \|q\|_Q &= \left( \int_{\Omega} q(x,y)^2 \, d\Omega \right)^{1/2} = \left|q(x,y) \approx \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N_p}p_i\psi_i(x,y)\right|=\\
        &= \left( \int_{\Omega} \sum_{i=1}^{N_p}p_i\psi_i(x,y) \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_j\psi_j(x,y)\, d\Omega \right)^{1/2} = \left( \int_{\Omega} \sum_{i=1}^{N_p}\sum_{j=1}^{N_p}p_i p_j\psi_j(x,y)\psi_i(x,y)\, d\Omega \right)^{1/2} = \\
        &= \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N_p}\sum_{j=1}^{N_p} p_i \cdot \left(\int_{\Omega} \psi_j(x,y)\psi_i(x,y)\, d\Omega\right) \cdot p_j\right)^{1/2} = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N_p}\sum_{j=1}^{N_p} p_i \cdot \left(M_p\right)_{ij} \cdot p_j\right)^{1/2} = \left(\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{M}_p \mathbf{p}\right)^{1/2}
    \end{aligned}$
\end{enumerate}
\normalsize

\subsection{The Final Discrete Matrix Form}
By substituting our three derived algebraic expressions directly back into the continuous supremum framework, the continuous inf-sup condition simplifies into its final, discrete matrix form:

\[
\exists \beta>0: \quad
\inf_{\mathbf{p} \not= \mathbf{0}} \space \sup_{\mathbf{u} \not= \mathbf{0}} \left(\frac{\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B} \mathbf{u}}{\left(\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{M}_v \mathbf{u}\right)^{1/2} \cdot \left(\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{M}_p \mathbf{p}\right)^{1/2}}\right) \ge \beta \\[10pt]
\]

This discrete fraction acts as a normalized compatibility test, using $\mathbf{M}_v$ and $\mathbf{M}_p$ to ensure that the fields are measured by their true physical sizes rather than arbitrary numbers. Because calculating a nested maximum and minimum over thousands of mesh nodes is impossible for a computer, we must use a mathematical shortcut to transform this fraction into a simple matrix eigenvalue problem.

\subsection{Solving the Optimization Problem}
The optimization problem is of the following appearence:

\[
\min_{\mathbf{p} \not= \mathbf{0}} \space \max_{\mathbf{u} \not= \mathbf{0}} \left(\frac{\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B} \mathbf{u}}{\left(\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{M}_v \mathbf{u}\right)^{1/2} \cdot \left(\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{M}_p \mathbf{p}\right)^{1/2}}\right) = \min_{\mathbf{p} \not= \mathbf{0}} \space \max_{\mathbf{u} \not= \mathbf{0}} \left(  \gamma(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{p})\right)\\[10pt]
\]

We start with the maximization of $\gamma(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{p})$:

\[
\max_{\mathbf{u} \not= \mathbf{0}}\left( \gamma(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{p})\right) = \max_{\mathbf{u} \not= \mathbf{0}} \left(\frac{\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{B} \mathbf{u}}{\left(\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{M}_v \mathbf{u}\right)^{1/2} \cdot \left(\mathbf{p}^T \mathbf{M}_p \mathbf{p}\right)^{1/2}}\right)
\]

\subsection{First Einsum}
\label{sub:first_example}

\begin{lstlisting}[
    language=Python, 
    frame=single,                  
    backgroundcolor=\color{white}, 
    basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize, 
    breaklines=true                 
    Overfull \hbox
]
A_local = np.zeros(shape=(Nt, 3, 3))
for i in range(3):
    for j in range(i, 3):

        grad_i = test_function_derivatives[i]
        grad_j = test_function_derivatives[j]

        val = np.einsum('i,nij,j->n', 
                        grad_j, Q_mat, grad_i)

        val *= kinematic_viscosity / (2 * det_J)

        A_local[:, i, j] = val
        A_local[:, j, i] = val
\end{lstlisting}

\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
    \left[\psi_1, \psi_2\right]  
    & 
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        q^n_{11} & q^n_{12} \\
        q^n_{21} & q^n_{22}
    \end{array}
    \right]
    & \left[\psi_1, \psi_2\right] \\
    \psi_i & q^n_{ij} & \psi_j
\end{array}
\]

\[
\sum_{i=2}^2\sum_{j=2}^2\psi_i q^n_{ij} \psi_j = A^n \in \mathbb{R}^n
\]

\section{Numerical Experiments and Applications}
In this section, we validate our $P_1\text{-iso-}P_1$ finite element solver using two distinct fluid engineering case studies: an \textit{Exchanger Device} and a \textit{Winding Pipe}. Both configurations operate under a creeping flow regime ($\mathrm{Re} \ll 1$), where viscous forces dominate. 

For each case study, the numerical results are presented in a vertical sequence showing:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pressure Field Contour Maps:} Tri-contour plots displaying the continuous linear scalar pressure fields ($\mathbf{P}$) and revealing the pressure drop across the domains.
    \item \textbf{Velocity Field Streamplots:} Streamline trajectories displaying the path lines, vector flow patterns, and boundary layer behaviors of the velocity field ($\mathbf{u}$).
\end{enumerate}

The numerical results inside the \textit{Exchanger Device} reveal distinct kinematic adjustments driven by the variable cross-sectional area of the channel network. As observed in the velocity streamline distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:exchanger_streamlines}), the fluid velocity increases significantly at the turns, where the geometry narrows relative to the wider vertical passages. To preserve mass conservation under the incompressibility constraint $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$, the velocity field must accelerate through these local contractions, creating higher localized velocity gradients along the curved inner walls.

Furthermore, the simulation clearly shows fluid swirls (recirculation zones) inside the dead-end, cut-off extensions. Because Stokes flow has no inertia, the fluid balance simplifies to a strict equilibrium between pressure gradients and viscous friction:
\[
-\nu \Delta \mathbf{u} + \nabla p = \mathbf{0}
\]
As a result, the main stream takes the path of least resistance through the primary central channels. However, as this fast-moving core flow sweeps past the stagnant fluid trapped in the side cavities, it shears against it. This boundary shear transfers momentum across the open gap, dragging the passive fluid along and creating slow, circular vortices inside the extensions. Capturing these delicate stagnation zones and sharp shear interfaces demonstrates that the mixed finite element solver handles localized flow splitting accurately without generating numerical instability.
